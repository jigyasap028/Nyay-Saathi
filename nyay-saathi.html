File an FIR — do this today

First Information Report · Section 154 CrPC · Your most important first action

Step by step — what to do now
1
Go to the police station where the crime occurredYou must go to the station in whose area (jurisdiction) the crime took place. The station in-charge is legally required to register your FIR under Section 154 CrPC. They cannot legally refuse.
2
Give your complaint in writing, or tell it out loudIf you give it orally, the officer must write it down themselves, read it back to you, and have you sign it. You are legally entitled to a free copy of the FIR immediately — ask for it.
3
What to bringAny Aadhaar or identity card. Any evidence — photos, screenshots, medical certificate if injured. Names and phone numbers of any witnesses. Write down the exact time, date, place, and what happened before you go.
4
If police refuse to register your FIRSend a written complaint by post to the Superintendent of Police (SP) of the district. You can also approach a Judicial Magistrate directly under Section 156(3) CrPC and request them to direct the police to file the FIR.
5
If you need a lawyer and cannot afford oneContact your District Legal Services Authority (DLSA). Every district has one. Free legal aid is your right under the Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987. They can assign you a lawyer at no cost.
ℹ️

Remember: The FIR is a public document and yours by right. Once registered, insist on getting a stamped copy. This is the foundation of your entire case — keep it safe.

You can still file an FIR

There is no fixed time limit to register an FIR · Delay may need explanation, but your right remains

Step by step
1
Go to the relevant police station and file your complaintThe same right to file an FIR exists regardless of delay. However, be prepared to explain why time passed — document the reason (fear, illness, travel, not knowing your rights).
2
Preserve whatever evidence you still haveScreenshots, messages, medical reports, photos, witness contacts. Even if time has passed, evidence can be collected now. Courts take delayed reporting seriously when explained honestly.
3
If police cite delay as a reason to refuseThis is not legally valid. You can approach the SP by post, or file a private complaint before a Magistrate under Section 156(3) or Section 200 CrPC.
4
Consider approaching a legal aid lawyer firstA DLSA lawyer can help you draft a strong complaint that addresses the delay upfront — which increases your chances of the FIR being registered without resistance.
ℹ️

In cases of sexual violence, the Supreme Court has repeatedly held that delayed FIRs do not destroy credibility — courts are instructed to consider the trauma and social context of delay.

File now and seek protection

Ongoing harm · Protection orders available · You do not have to wait for it to stop

Step by step
1
File an FIR immediately for the most recent incidentYou do not need to wait for it to happen again. File for the most recent instance today. Each occurrence can be reported separately, or as a pattern of continuous conduct.
2
For domestic violence — the Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005 appliesYou can approach a Protection Officer (every district has one) or a Magistrate directly for a Protection Order, Residence Order, or Monetary Relief — without filing an FIR first. These are civil remedies and faster.
3
Keep a written record of each incidentDate, time, what happened, any witnesses, any injuries. Even a personal diary entry with dates has been accepted as corroboration by courts.
4
Contact a women's / victims' helplineDial 181 (Women Helpline), 112 (Emergency), or 1091 (Women in Distress). Legal aid NGOs can also provide immediate support and legal assistance.
ℹ️

You are safe to report. Under the DV Act, a Protection Officer must assist you in filing a complaint. If police are unresponsive, go directly to a Magistrate. The law creates this alternative path precisely for situations where fear or dependency makes police reporting difficult.

Act immediately — rights are time-sensitive

Article 22 of the Constitution · Fundamental rights on arrest

What the law guarantees — right now
1
They must be told why they were arrestedArticle 22(1) of the Constitution: every arrested person must be informed of the grounds of arrest immediately. This is not optional for the police — it is a fundamental right.
2
They must be produced before a Magistrate within 24 hoursArticle 22(2): if the police fail to do this, the detention becomes unlawful. You can immediately file a Habeas Corpus petition in the High Court if this right is being violated.
3
They have the right to consult a lawyer immediatelyArticle 22(1): they can consult a lawyer of their choice from the moment of arrest. Police cannot deny this. If they cannot afford a lawyer, contact the District Legal Services Authority (DLSA) immediately — free aid is available as a right.
4
A family member or friend must be informedSection 50A CrPC requires the police to inform a nominated person of the arrest and the place of detention. Ask the arresting officer directly — insist on this right.
5
Find out exactly where they are being heldGo to the nearest police station. Ask for the "production register" or "lock-up register." You have the right to know the place of detention.
📞

Call your District Legal Services Authority (DLSA) now. They are legally required to provide emergency legal aid and can send a lawyer to the police station. This is free. Find your DLSA at nalsa.gov.in or call 15100 (NALSA helpline).

⚖️

You have three formal paths

Against police excess or government rights violations

Choose based on urgency and severity
1
State Human Rights Commission (SHRC) — free, no lawyer neededFile a written complaint at your state's SHRC. No fee, no lawyer required. You can submit in Hindi or English. They can recommend compensation, inquiry against officers, and corrective action.
2
National Human Rights Commission (NHRC) — for serious or unresolved casesFile online at nhrc.nic.in. Also free. If the SHRC is unresponsive or the violation is grave (custodial death, torture, forced confession), NHRC has broader authority. They can issue notices to Chief Secretaries and DGPs.
3
Writ Petition — High Court under Article 226For urgent or serious violations, a writ petition in the High Court is the most powerful remedy. A Habeas Corpus can be filed the same day. DLSA will assign a free lawyer to help you do this.
📋

Before you do anything: document everything. Write down dates, officer names and badge numbers, witnesses, what was said or done. Get a medical certificate immediately if there was any physical harm. These documents are the basis of every path above.

Start with free legal advice

Your situation may need a personalised path — here's how to get one at no cost

Where to go first
1
District Legal Services Authority (DLSA)Every district in India has a DLSA. Walk in with your problem — in any language. They provide free legal advice and can assign a lawyer if you qualify. No paperwork needed to just ask for advice.
2
NALSA Helpline — 15100Call 15100 (toll-free) to reach the National Legal Services Authority. They can direct you to the right authority for your specific situation and connect you with local legal aid services.
3
Lok Adalat — for disputes and settlementsIf your matter involves a dispute (money, property, family) that both parties might want to resolve without a long court case, a Lok Adalat can resolve it free, fast, and finally — with the force of a court decree.
